## The symptom pattern

At the end of a cut the lower guard is supposed to snap back over the blade
the instant the saw clears the wood. Instead it drifts back lazily, or stops
halfway, or waits for a flick of the retract lever. Owners get used to it by
degrees, which is exactly the problem — this fault has a habit of being
tolerated right up until a still-spinning, unguarded blade meets a thigh or a
bench.

It's worth stating plainly: a slow lower guard is not a cosmetic annoyance.
It's the one fault on this page that can put you in hospital while the saw
itself runs perfectly.

## What's actually happening

The lower guard rotates on a ring around the blade housing, pulled shut by a
torsion spring. That ring gap sits directly in the path of the dust stream,
and softwood resin plus fine dust bakes into a sticky varnish inside it. The
spring only has so much torque; as the varnish builds, the guard slows, then
sticks. The 5704R is a workhorse saw that tends to see years of softwood and
sheet material, so most examples get here eventually.

The less common causes are mechanical: a return spring that has stretched or
jumped its seat, or a guard rim dented in a drop so it scrapes the housing at
one point in its travel — the drag in that case is felt at a specific spot
rather than evenly.

## The fix

With the saw unplugged, clean the pivot ring gap thoroughly: resin solvent or
white spirit on a brush, worked in while cycling the guard, until it moves
freely, then blow it dry. Lubricate with a dry PTFE or silicone spray only.
Oil and grease feel like an improvement for a week, then collect dust into a
paste and recreate the fault worse than before.

If the guard is free but still lazy, replace the return spring — it's on the
parts diagram and costs little. A bent guard should be replaced rather than
bent back; its geometry is what keeps it clear of the blade.

## Verdict

Ten minutes with solvent fixes almost every case, and the parts for the rest
are cheap. The verdict that matters is the safety one: this is a
stop-using-the-saw fault until fixed, and wedging or tying the guard open is
never an acceptable workaround — that modification has a well-documented
casualty record.
