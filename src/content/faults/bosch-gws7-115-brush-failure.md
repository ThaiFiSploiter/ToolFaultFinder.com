## The symptom pattern

The grinder gets unreliable before it gets dead. It starts fine most days,
then one morning needs the trigger cycled a few times, or a tap on the body,
or starts only from certain spindle positions. Under load it may cut out for
a second and come back. Eventually it won't start at all. That slow decline
is the signature of brushes reaching the end of their travel.

## What's actually happening

Like the vast majority of angle grinders, the GWS 7-115 uses a universal
motor: carbon brushes press against the spinning commutator to feed current
into the armature. Brushes are a designed-in consumable — they wear down
millimetre by millimetre over the tool's life. When they get short, spring
pressure can no longer hold them firmly against the commutator, and contact
becomes position-dependent: the motor starts from some spindle angles and not
others, which is exactly what the wiggle test detects.

Some brushes include an auto-stop feature — an insert that cuts contact
entirely at the wear limit — in which case the tool goes from working to
stone dead without the intermittent phase.

## Working through it

Check the cable before opening anything: flex the cord where it enters the
tool and the plug while holding the switch on. Cord conductors break at
exactly those two points and produce identical intermittent symptoms for even
less money than brushes.

Inside, the diagnosis is visual: brushes worn near their limit, sticking in
their holders, or with collapsed heat-blued springs condemn themselves.
While they're out, look at the commutator — an even, polished
copper-to-light-brown surface is healthy; heavy blackening or grooving means
the worn brushes have been arcing and the commutator may need attention too
(see our Triton TPT125 entry for that diagnosis).

If the brushes come out long, springy, and free-sliding, move suspicion to
the switch — the other classic wear point on grinders, and equally cheap.

## Verdict

Brushes are pennies, sold as pairs, and always replaced as pairs. Run the
grinder unloaded for a few minutes afterwards to bed the new faces to the
commutator before grinding anything. This is the definitive first repair to
learn on any brushed power tool — cheap, low-risk, and it works.
