## The symptom pattern

In thin material the saw cuts fine. In an 18mm worktop or a 45mm softwood
board, the cut drifts off the line, and when you look at the cut edge it's
not vertical — the blade has leaned over inside the material, sometimes
badly enough that the underside of the cut is millimetres away from where
the top says it should be. Setting the base plate to exactly 90° changes
nothing, which is the clue that this isn't an adjustment problem.

## What's actually happening

A jigsaw blade is a thin strip of steel supported only at the top. In thick
material, side forces — a dull edge cutting unevenly, grain steering the
teeth, the user pushing the saw back onto a drifting line — flex the free
end of the blade sideways. The base plate stays perfectly square while the
blade bends beneath it; the bevel is created inside the cut. Deeper stock
means a longer unsupported blade and more leverage, which is why the fault
scales with thickness.

Everything that resists deflection helps: a stiffer, wider blade, sharp
teeth that cut rather than push, a slow feed that doesn't load the blade,
and a healthy support roller backing the blade up. Everything that
aggravates it — the worn general-purpose blade in the saw since last year, a
fast feed, steering mid-cut — makes the lean worse.

## Working through it

The blade is the suspect until proven otherwise: sight down it for a set
bend (a blade that's been leaned on stays bent, and every subsequent cut
follows the bend), and start any diagnosis with a new blade of the correct
type for the material and thickness. Check the support roller behind the
blade — on a well-used saw the blade can wear a groove into the roller face
or the roller can seize, and either lets the blade wander unsupported.

Then test honestly: fresh stiff blade, thick scrap, slow feed, no steering.
If that edge comes out square, the technique and blade were the whole story.

## Verdict

Blades and patience, not repairs. Buy proper blades for thick stock, replace
them before they're fully dull, let the saw set the pace, and treat the
support roller as the one part worth inspecting. And if the job is a long
straight cut in thick worktop, that's a circular saw's job — a jigsaw will
always be the wrong tool doing its best.
