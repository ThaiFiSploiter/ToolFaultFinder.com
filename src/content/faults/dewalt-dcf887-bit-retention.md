## The symptom pattern

The driver runs perfectly, but bits no longer lock in with the positive click
they had when the tool was new. They rock visibly under load, slip partway
out, or in the worst case drop free mid-fastener. It creeps up gradually on
heavily used drivers, which is why it's often blamed on cheap bits long after
the chuck has become the real problem.

## What's actually happening

A quarter-inch hex quick-release chuck holds the bit with a spring-loaded
ball that sits in the groove of the bit shank; pulling the collar retracts
the ball to release. Every insertion drags grit through the mechanism, and an
impact driver adds thousands of hammer blows per minute on top. Eventually
the ball, its spring, or the groove it rides in wears to the point where the
grip goes mushy.

There are two impostors worth eliminating first. Worn bits — shanks with
rounded retention grooves — slip in a perfectly healthy chuck, and bulk-pack
bits get there quickly. And a chuck packed with compacted dust behaves
exactly like a worn one, because the ball can't seat fully.

## Working through it

Start with a fresh quality bit and the tug test: insert it without touching
the collar, then pull. A healthy chuck holds until the collar is pulled; a
bit that slides straight out condemns the retention mechanism. Sticky or
gritty collar action points at contamination, so clean the bore — compressed
air, a solvent-dampened swab around the ball, and a single drop of light oil
worked in by cycling the collar. A meaningful share of "worn" chucks come
back to life at this step.

If retention is still weak after cleaning, the wear is real. On the DCF887
the chuck components are serviceable: the parts diagram identifies the nose
assembly, and replacement is a bench job rather than a workshop one.

## Verdict

Rule out bits, then rule out dirt — both are free. A genuinely worn chuck is
a cheap, worthwhile repair on a driver of this quality. The one false economy
is ignoring it: a bit that drops out under torque eventually does so into
your workpiece, or your hand.
